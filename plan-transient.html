What kind of site is this?
Bats have fidelity to transient sites, so they will probably continue coming back year after year. To address this problem, first decide whether the roost is a natural site or a man-made site, because that changes whether the goal is conservation, monitoring, or intervention.
Natural site
Natural sites should usually be protected. These sites can also be used for controlled education and viewing, disturbance should be minimized.
Man-made site
Man-made sites may create conflict if bats use buildings, bridges, or other human structures, causing maintence issues, especially since they tend to return.
Conserve and protect
If the bats are using a natural site, the best response is usually conservation rather than removal because removal may cause it to disturb man-made spaces. Also, since they are an important part to the ecosystem, lethal method are not the best option.
Does the site need bat repelling?
Decide whether this site truly needs intervention. If the site causes major conflict, action may be needed. If not, monitoring and warning the public may be enough.
Yes: Identify what time in the year the bats arrive and leave at this place
If you plan to intervene, timing is critical. You need to know the seasonal window when bats arrive and leave so you can avoid trapping them and plan relocation properly.
No: Identify what time in the year the bats arrive and leave at this place. Warn civilians about bat presence in the area during migration season, and monitor bat activity.
If removal is not necessary, still identify the migration period. Then warn people about bat presence, reduce unnecessary alarm, and monitor whether the site becomes a problem later.
Use this information to organize seasonal, local bat sightseeing educational tours
Knowing when bats arrive and leave can help schedule local bat-viewing events and educational tours. This supports both conservation awareness and community engagement, and provides economical benefits.
The bats are still here
If the bats are still occupying the site when action is needed, specialists may need to move them carefully and clean the original site to remove scent cues that bats use to locate the site.
Hire specialists to remove the bats to a nearby alternative roosting site and clean up the original site thoroughly
Professional removal is safer than amateur attempts. Pair removal with a nearby alternative roost so the bats are redirected rather than simply displaced. Clean to remove the scent cues, lowering chances of bats coming back. Lethal methods do not work well because bats may still continuously come throughout migration.
The bats have left
If the bats have already left for the season, the site can be cleaned and prepared before they return.
Remove bat material to a nearby alternative site, and clean up the original site thoroughly
Clean the original site thoroughly and reduce the cues that might attract bats back. If appropriate, place attractants (bat products) at a more suitable nearby site, because these bats may use these chemical cues to guide themselves.
Next year before migration season, set up bat repellents at the original site and attract bats to a new roost site
Before bats return next season, prepare the original site so it is less attractive and guide bats toward a more suitable alternative roost.
