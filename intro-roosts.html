Roosts & Habitat

A roost is where bats rest, shelter, and gather. Roost choice matters because roosts shape colony size, reproduction, and migration stopovers — and they are also one of the main reasons bats come into conflict with humans.

What makes a good roost?

  • Dark or dim
  • Not frequently disturbed
  • Many cracks and crevices for clinging and hiding

Common roost types

Natural sites

  • Caves
  • Rock crevices

Natural roosts can support large colonies and can be important habitat worth protecting.

Man-made sites

  • Abandoned mines
  • Bridges
  • Culverts
  • Buildings (attics, walls, gaps)

Human-built roosts are where most nuisance conflicts happen, especially when bats get into living or high-traffic spaces.

Guano (bat droppings)

Guano is bat droppings. Guano can be beneficial (supports cave ecosystems and can be used as fertilizer), but it can also be dangerous in some contexts, especially if dry guano is disturbed and becomes airborne.

Managing roost access and educating people about safe cleanup and avoidance can reduce risk and fear.

Roost fidelity

Mexican free-tailed bats can show fidelity to roost sites (they return to the same places). This helps bats efficiently find safe shelters, but it can create recurring seasonal conflict when the roost is a building or structure people use.

Social behavior at roosts

Roosts aren’t just resting places — they’re also the center of social life. Mexican free-tailed bats can form extremely large colonies, and living in groups changes both their benefits and risks.

How they behave in groups

  • Huge colonies: colonies can range from tens to millions of bats.
  • Roostmate recognition: bats can recognize roostmates using scent (chemical cues).
  • Communication: they use social vocalizations to communicate, including locating offspring.

Why roosting in colonies matters

  • Benefits: more mating opportunities and social coordination.
  • Costs: higher exposure to parasites and disease in dense colonies.
  • Conflict link: big colonies + repeated site use can lead to major guano buildup and human complaints.
This is why roost-site management is so important: if a roost is safe and undisturbed, bats return — but if it’s a building or bridge used by people, the same social behaviors can create recurring seasonal conflict.
