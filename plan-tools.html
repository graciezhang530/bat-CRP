Tools & Methods We Use

This page explains the practical tools used in our conflict reduction plan. The goal is to reduce conflict without harming bats: we try to discourage roosting in high-conflict locations and provide safer alternatives nearby.

Two main tool categories: Bat repellants and Alternative roosting (bat houses).

1) Bat repeller

Deterrence tools are used when bats are roosting in places that create high conflict (for example, homes or busy public structures). In our plan, deterrence is not used alone, it is paired with prevention and an alternative roost so bats have a place to go.

Examples

  • Ultrasonic deterrents Ultrasonic bat repellents producing certain frequencies have been proven to be effective
  • Site modification Bat proofing mechanisms have been a widely applied mechanism by denying bat access (seal entry points, cover gaps, reduce roost access)
  • Chemical repellant Chemicals like Naphthalene have been applied and proven to be effective.
  • LightMany have taken the appraoch of making the roost uncomfortable by using light, since bats prefer dark, quiet places to roost
  • Most of these devices cost only 10-20 dollars

How we use it

  1. Confirm the site is high-conflict and action is necessary.
  2. Use repellants to reduce roost attractiveness.
  3. At the same time, prepare an alternative roost site nearby (bat house).
  4. Monitor: do bats stop returning? do they shift to the alternative roost?
Reminder: deterrence works best as part of a system (deterrence + prevention + alternative roost), not as a one-step “scare them away” approach.

2) Bat houses (alternative roost sites)

Bat houses are a conflict-reduction tool because they provide bats with a safer roost option away from high-conflict buildings. They are especially useful when you’re trying to guide bats away from a problem location without removing bats from the area entirely.

Design features (from our slides)

  • Taller, rectangular designs work better than short/wide boxes.
  • Multiple chambers support different temperature zones and colony sizes.
  • Rough interior surfaces help bats cling.
  • Mounted higher off the ground (safer and more attractive).
  • Lower predator access by placement and mounting.
  • Color and sun exposure should match local climate (temperature control).
  • Cost ranges from 20 - 100 dollars.

How we use it

  1. Place bat houses near the area bats already use, but some distance away from high-conflict buildings.
  2. Pair placement with reduced access/attractiveness at the original site.
  3. Monitor uptake: look for emergence, guano underneath, or consistent bat presence.

How tools connect to our three plan parts

Roosting problem

Use deterrence + prevention when needed, and provide bat houses to redirect roosting away from homes/structures.
