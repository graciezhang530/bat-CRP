Plan Overview

This conflict reduction plan is designed to help humans and Mexican free-tailed bats coexist more safely. Mexican free-tailed bats can form very large colonies and may roost in both natural sites and human-built structures. When conflicts happen, people may try to remove bats in harmful ways or react out of fear. This plan organizes clear steps to reduce conflict while protecting bats and maintaining public safety.

Big idea: We address conflict from three angles — where bats roost, how people perceive bats, and what happens during migration when bats may use transient stopover sites.

Why we need to do this

What can go wrong without a plan?

  • Roosting conflicts: bats in buildings can create guano/odor cleanup problems and lead to unsafe removal attempts.
  • Fear and misinformation: stigma can cause bats to be harmed or killed, and also put people in risk.
  • Migration peaks: transient stopover sites can bring repeat seasonal conflict if timing and prevention aren’t planned.

What this plan aims to achieve

  • Protect bats: reduce disturbance and avoid harmful handling or removal.
  • Protect people: clear guidance for safe responses and reduced nuisance in public spaces.
  • Reduce repeat conflicts: prevention, timing, and alternatives so the same problem doesn’t return every year.
