Migration

Migration patterns vary between populations: some bats migrate long distances, some migrate shorter distances, and some remain in the same region year-round.

Typical seasonal pattern (slide summary)

  • Spring: migrate north for mating
  • Summer: nurse pups in the north (maternity colonies)
  • Fall: after weaning, migrate south
  • Winter: stay in large mixed-sex colonies in the south
Key idea: migration is tied to reproduction and roost availability, so timing matters for any management decisions.

Transient roosts (stopover sites)

During migration, bats may use transient roosts as temporary stopovers. These sites can be important for navigation and recovery. Bats may show fidelity to these sites, meaning they may return in future migration seasons.

This matters for humans because a “temporary” bat presence can repeat every year at the same site if the site remains attractive.

How migration links to conflict

What people experience

  • Seasonal spikes in bat presence
  • Unexpected roosting in structures (bridges/buildings)
  • Repeated disturbance seasonally

What the plan does about it

  • Identify when bats arrive/leave
  • Monitor activity during migration windows
  • Prepare the site before next season (reduce attraction + guide to alternative roosts)
