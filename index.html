Overview

Mexican Free-tailed Bat
Tadarida brasiliensis

This website presents a conflict reduction plan focused on three issues between humans and Mexican free-tailed bats: (1) roosting conflicts in human structures, (2) public stigma and fear, and (3) migration-related transient stopover sites that can cause recurring seasonal disturbance.

Project Goals

Bat side

  • Maintain safe and stable roost opportunities across seasons
  • Avoid harm from fear-driven responses
  • Keep transient guidance sites available during migration

Human side

  • Keep indoor public spaces bat-free to reduce maintenance conflicts
  • Have a correct understanding of bats
  • Reduce disturbance during migration peaks

Need for conservation

  • Loss of roosts due to guano mining and the burning or dynamiting of roosts
  • Pesticides have caused decline in bat populations
  • An adult Mexican free-tailed bat can consume 8.1 g of insects each night
  • Mexican free-tailed bats provide pest-suppression services in the southwestern U.S. and northern Mexico valued at about $11.67 million USD per year
  • Research shows that U.S. and Mexican households are willing to pay an average of $30 each year to protect the Mexican free-tailed bat
  • Bat caves are of great economic value, gaining about $6.5 million in consumer surplus annually

→ Mexican free-tailed bats are decreasing in number. Since they are important insectivores and contributors to ecotourism, loss of these bats may cause major economic losses. Thus, the best solution is to resolve the conflict between humans and bats rather than using lethal methods.

Plan at a Glance

1) Roosting Problem

Classify the roost (natural vs man-made), decide if exclusion is needed, prioritize prevention (seal entry points), and use humane one-way exclusion only when appropriate.

Read Roosting Plan

2) Public Stigma

Reduce fear-driven conflict with clear education: myth vs fact, safe response steps, signage where conflict occurs, and outreach linking to this website.

Read Stigma Plan

3) Migration & Transient Sites

Identify transient stopovers with short monitoring, time interventions, and provide alternative roosts to redirect.
