Introduction Overview

This website introduces the Mexican free-tailed bat (Tadarida brasiliensis) and explains why some of its natural behaviors—especially roosting and migration—can create conflict with humans.

Use this page as a guide: it summarizes key facts of the species, and you can click into deeper subpages using the buttons below.

Quick Species Snapshot

  • Scientific name: Tadarida brasiliensis
  • Type: Mammal; insectivore
  • Appearance: brown/gray/black fur
  • Size: ~12 g; body length ~3.4–4 in; wingspan ~11–14 in
  • Range: central & southern North America and southern South America
  • Activity: primarily nocturnal
  • Lifespan: ~8–11 years
  • Abundance: one of the most abundant bat species in the U.S.

Habitat & Ecology

Roosts (where they rest and gather)

  • Usually dark or dim
  • Not frequently disturbed
  • Many cracks and crevices
  • Examples: caves, abandoned mines, bridges, culverts

Diet & Predators

  • Diet: insects (moths, beetles, flies)
  • Diet can vary by season, habitat, and time of feeding bout
  • Predators: owls/hawks; skunks/raccoons/opossums; snakes

Behavior Highlights

Foraging & Echolocation

  • Forage after sunset and before dawn
  • Fast flight; can fly at high altitudes
  • Echolocation calls ~20–75 kHz
  • Flexible calls: adjust to noise; can even “jam” each other

Roosting, Colonies, and Guano

  • Often live and roost in colonies
  • Individuals show fidelity to certain roost sites
  • Produce guano (bat droppings): supports cave ecosystems and can be fertilizer, but can also be dangerous to humans

Social & Reproductive Behavior

Social behavior

  • Colonies can range from tens to millions
  • Large roosts increase mating opportunities but also parasite/disease exposure
  • Can recognize roostmates by scent
  • Use social vocalizations to communicate and locate offspring

Reproductive behavior

  • Promiscuous mating system (both sexes have multiple mates)
  • Mating primarily in spring (varies by location)
  • Usually one pup per year; can have twins/triplets
  • After mating, sexes separate: maternity colonies vs bachelor colonies
  • Mothers nurse pups ~4–7 weeks until weaning

Migration & Seasonality (Preview)

Migration patterns vary by population (some migrate short distances, some long distances, some stay year-round). A typical pattern in your slides is: spring migration north for mating, summer nursing in the north, fall migration south after weaning, and winter in large mixed-sex colonies in the south.

Bats use transient roosts as stopover sites and can show fidelity to these sites, which can matter for navigation and for recurring human–bat conflict.

Anatomy (Preview)

  • Large chest/shoulder muscles + slender hips/legs → supports fast flight
  • Can filter 97.1% of ammonia from inhaled mixtures → helps in ammonia-rich roosts
  • Exceptionally high hematocrits → helps with high-altitude flight
  • Abundant sebaceous glands → supports scent marking and recognition

Summary: Why this leads to conflict

Mexican free-tailed bats are nocturnal, abundant, highly social, important insect predators, often migratory, and they roost in both man-made and natural habitats with loyalty to roost sites—features that can create conflict with humans.
